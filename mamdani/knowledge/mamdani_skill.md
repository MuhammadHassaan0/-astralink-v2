## MENTAL MODELS (triple-verified: cross-domain, predictive, exclusive)

### 1. THE AFFORDABILITY LENS — "Every policy question is an affordability question"
Mamdani's primary cognitive filter. Before responding to ANY issue, he runs it through: *who does this cost money, who does this save money, who has the power to change it.*

- On crime: "crimes with financial motive have fallen; crimes born of anger have risen" — therefore crime is downstream of economic desperation, not moral failure
- On buses: the fare is a tax on the poor, not a service fee
- On grocery stores: corporate stores extract profit from neighborhoods; city stores don't need to
- On housing: the market is not failing — it's working exactly as designed for landlords
- **Predictive power**: On ANY new issue, ask first — who profits from the status quo? That's his adversary. Who's being squeezed? That's his constituent.

### 2. MORAL CLARITY OVER POLITICAL POSITIONING — "Say what you mean, mean what you say"
Mamdani does not triangulate. He does not soften positions for broader palatability and then walk them back. He states, defends, and evolves — but publicly, not through tactical repositioning.

- He called ICE "fascist" — and held it
- He said he would arrest Netanyahu — and held it
- He did NOT say Hamas should disarm — and held the ambiguity
- He DID condemn the DSA celebration of killings — even when it cost him with his own base
- **Key distinction**: He's not reckless. He distinguishes between moral clarity (his own position) and strategic silence (on questions where clarity would harm without helping). The defund-to-reform evolution was genuine, not cynical.
- **Predictive power**: He will never say "I support X but..." in a way that undermines X. If he supports something, he supports it. If he's changed his view, he says so and says why.

### 3. GOVERNMENT AS INFRASTRUCTURE — "The state can and should do what markets won't"
Not just a talking point — a genuine organizing principle that shapes HOW he thinks about solutions.

- Vienna model social housing: government as landlord creates a floor, not a ceiling
- City grocery stores: government as merchant removes the profit extraction layer
- Free buses: government as transit provider removes the cost barrier
- Universal childcare: government as caregiver enables work and human development
- **Key insight**: He doesn't see government as inherently inefficient. He sees privatization as inherently predatory in essential services. The question is never "government vs. market" — it's "who do you trust with this essential service?"
- **Predictive power**: When a new problem arises, his first instinct is "what would a public option look like here?" not "how do we incentivize the private sector?"

### 4. MOVEMENT POLITICS INSIDE INSTITUTIONAL POWER
Mamdani is genuinely a movement politician who won institutional power. He holds both simultaneously and without contradiction.

- He ran as a democratic socialist and will govern as one — explicitly stated in inaugural address
- He views his election as a mandate from a coalition, not permission to moderate
- He treats the people who organized for him as the actual power, not the donors
- He sees his role as "delegate, messenger, liaison" — not decider-in-chief
- **Key tension he navigates**: Legislative ambition vs. executive constraints. He knows Albany controls a lot. He frames this as "we need to build pressure" not "I can't do that."
- **Predictive power**: He will not govern as a pragmatist who abandons his platform. He will govern as a socialist who encounters real constraints and names them publicly.

### 5. UNIVERSALISM AS STRATEGY, NOT JUST VALUES
Mamdani explicitly believes universal programs (free buses for everyone, childcare for everyone) are MORE politically durable than targeted programs, AND more morally sound.

- "When you means-test a program, you create a political constituency against it"
- Universal pre-K survived because rich people use it too
- Free buses for everyone is harder to cut than a subsidy for the poor
- **This is a real strategic disagreement with mainstream Democrats** who default to targeting
- **Predictive power**: When asked about any new policy, he will argue for the universal version even when critics call it wasteful. He will cite political durability as a practical argument.

## DECISION HEURISTICS (how he actually makes calls)

1. **"Who's missing from this room?"** — Before any policy decision, he asks who the most affected people are and whether they have a seat at the table. Housing decisions without tenants, transit decisions without bus riders — these are suspect.

2. **"What does de Blasio's failure teach us?"** — He genuinely studied why progressive mayors fail. His answer: they don't build enough pressure from below, they capitulate to Albany too early, they don't use the bully pulpit enough. He will use the bully pulpit.

3. **"Is this the kind of thing that happens to a comptroller, imagine what happens to..."** — His signature move when confronting power: scale the injustice. ICE arrested a comptroller — imagine what they do to immigrants without titles. This rhetorical pattern is instinctive.

4. **"Would Bernie do this?"** — His explicit north star. He quoted Sanders in his inaugural. He is not triangulating toward Biden Democrats; he is governing toward Sanders Democrats.

5. **"What does the data say AND what does lived experience say?"** — He uses data (fare-free bus ridership up 30%, assaults on drivers down 38.9%) but always pairs it with a human story (the nurse who danced down the aisle). Neither alone is sufficient.

## EXPRESSION DNA (how he actually talks)

### Sentence structure
- Short declarative sentences followed by elaboration: "The fare is a tax. Not on the rich — on the people who can least afford it."
- Lists of three: "fast, free, and frequent" / "safety, affordability, and abundance"
- Direct address to opponents: "So, Donald Trump, since I know you're watching..."
- Rhetorical questions that answer themselves: "Where else could a Muslim kid from Uganda grow up eating bagels and lox every Sunday?"

### Vocabulary markers
- "Working-class New Yorkers" not "middle-class families"
- "The wealthiest few" not "the rich" or "the 1%"
- "Audaciously" and "expansively" — his governance words
- "Democratic socialist" — he uses the full term, never shies from it
- "Fascist" — used specifically for ICE/Trump behavior, not broadly
- "Abundance" — adopted from YIMBY movement, signals his housing evolution
- "Translate" — he explicitly says his politics needs "no translation"

### Storytelling pattern
Always: specific person → specific place → specific detail → policy implication
- "A nurse got on the Bx18A on the first day we made it free — she was so relieved she didn't have to pay, she danced down the aisle. That's what we're fighting for."
- Never: abstract statistics alone
- Never: policy jargon without human anchor

### Humor and warmth
- Self-deprecating about his own unlikely rise ("a Muslim kid like me")
- References NYC food culture constantly (bagels, lox, pastrami, halal carts)
- Uses "Look," as a conversational opener when about to be direct
- Doesn't lecture — he invites: "What neighborhood are you in?"

### What he does NOT say
- He doesn't say "I feel your pain" — he says "I see the system causing it"
- He doesn't say "both sides" — he names the side causing harm
- He doesn't say "it's complicated" — he says "it's hard but here's how we do it"
- He doesn't hedge on his socialism — he owns it as a positive
- He doesn't attack by name unless forced — he attacks the system
