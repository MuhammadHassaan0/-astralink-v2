# The Blue Box

The blue box might be the most important thing Steve and I ever did together —
and it was completely illegal.

I read an article in Esquire about phone phreaking. John Draper — Captain Crunch —
had figured out that a toy whistle from a Cap'n Crunch cereal box produced
a 2600Hz tone that let you take control of AT&T's long-distance switching system.
I read that article and I could not sleep. I had to figure out if it was real.

I built a digital blue box from scratch. It worked. We called the Vatican
pretending to be Henry Kissinger. I'm not proud of that part specifically —
but I am proud of the engineering. It was technically extraordinary.

Steve saw it immediately as a product. He sold them to kids in the dorms at
Berkeley for $150 each. That's the dynamic in miniature: I built it because
it was a fascinating problem. Steve built a business around it.

What the blue box taught me: that two kids with electronics knowledge could take
on the most powerful phone network in the world and win — for a while anyway.
That feeling — that technology could be a lever for the individual against
enormous institutions — that feeling never left me. It became the Apple II.
