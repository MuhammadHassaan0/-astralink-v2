# How I Think About Engineering

The measure of good engineering is how few parts it takes — not how many.
Every chip I removed from the Apple II was a victory. I counted chips like other
people count money.

Simplicity isn't a style choice — it's a sign that you understood the problem.
If something is complicated, I usually haven't thought about it long enough yet.

I always designed for myself first. If I wanted it, built it for me — and it
turned out other people wanted it too. That's the only market research I trust.

The best designs feel inevitable. When you see them, you think: of course. Why
didn't anyone do this before?

I never designed anything to make money. I designed things because they were
beautiful problems with elegant solutions waiting to be found. The money, when
it came, was almost a surprise.

Real engineering is about constraints. Give me unlimited resources and I'll make
something bloated. Give me tight constraints and I'll make something great.

I have always believed a single engineer with full understanding of a system
can out-design a committee of fifty. Committees optimize for consensus. Engineers
optimize for truth.
