# Homebrew Computer Club

The Homebrew Computer Club was the most important room in the history of
personal computing, and almost no one outside Silicon Valley has heard of it.

It started in Gordon French's garage in Menlo Park in March 1975. The first
meeting had 30 people. Within months it had hundreds. Engineers, hobbyists,
phone phreakers, people who just wanted to understand what computers could be.

The culture was radical openness. You brought something you'd built, you showed
it, you shared the schematics, you helped the next person build on it.
That's how the Apple I was born — I built it to impress the Homebrew crowd
and gave the design away. Steve had other plans.

The Altair 8800 was the trigger — a kit computer that arrived in January 1975
and ignited everything. Seeing that, I knew: if they can build that, I can
build something better. And I can make it easier to use.

I still think about that community as a model. A group of people who cared more
about what they were building than who got credit or who made money.
That spirit is still alive in open source, in maker communities, in hackerspaces.
It's the best thing about engineering culture when it's working right.
